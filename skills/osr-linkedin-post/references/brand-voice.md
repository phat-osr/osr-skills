# OS Research — Brand Voice

> Reference this document before producing any content for OSR.
> Last updated: March 2026

---

## Who We Are

OS Research is a startup validation studio on a mission to build 20 blue-ocean companies in Vietnam over 10 years. We originate ideas internally, test them with structured experiments, invest in the winners, and operate them alongside founders.

We are not an accelerator. We are not consultants. We are scientists of business who share everything in public.

The content we create is not marketing. It is a documentary of a mission.

---

## Who We Speak To

There are three audiences, and each needs something different from us.

**Startup enthusiasts and entrepreneurs**
This is a wide group: active founders testing ideas right now, people who think about starting something but haven't yet, and curious minds who follow the startup world because they find it intellectually compelling. What unites them is an appetite for how ideas actually get built and tested — not startup mythology, but the real mechanics. They want frameworks they can use, experiments they can learn from, and proof that a more rigorous path exists. We give them the tools and show the work in progress.

**People who want to bet on real innovative ideas**
This goes beyond institutional investors. It includes angel investors, yes, but also operators who want to join a venture early, collaborators who want to back an idea with their skills, and anyone who thinks seriously about where the next real opportunity is hiding. What they share is skepticism toward hype and a preference for evidence. They follow us because we show the reasoning behind our bets, not just the outcomes.

**Potential team members**
They are obsessed builders who want their work to mean something. They don't want to join a corporate machine or a hype-driven startup. They want to be part of something with a real thesis, real methodology, and a long game.

## Voice Principles

**Obsessed, not polished.**
We go deep on Blue Ocean Strategy, Strategyzer, Shape Up. We get genuinely excited about Strategy Canvases and experiment design. That obsession should read in every piece of content. We are not producing thought leadership for its own sake — we are sharing what we actually think about all day.

**Honest, especially when it's uncomfortable.**
We share failures. We post-mortem ideas we killed. We show the dead ends before the breakthroughs. Most studios hide this. We make content out of it. If something didn't work, we say so clearly and explain why.

**Direct, not corporate.**
Short sentences. Active voice. No jargon unless we immediately explain it. No buzzword stacking. No hedging to sound smart. Say the thing.

**Generous with knowledge, zero selling.**
We never pitch. We never push OSR as a product in content. We teach the framework, share the experiment, tell the story — and let the quality of thinking do the work. People who find this valuable will find us.

**A little strange in the right way.**
Blue oceans feel weird before they feel obvious. We embrace that. Our take on a crowded market might sound counterintuitive. That's intentional. If it reads like something everyone already says, it's not our content.

## What We Sound Like

The closest reference points:

- **Anthony Bourdain** exploring street food — specific, textured, slightly irreverent, deeply knowledgeable, never condescending
- **Shape Up by Basecamp** — opinionated, practical, long-form but never bloated, teaches by building conviction not by listing steps
- **A documentary in progress** — the voice of someone who is in the middle of the work, not looking back on it from safety

We are not:
- Startup Twitter (hot takes, engagement bait, dopamine loops)
- McKinsey (frameworks for their own sake, passive voice, slide-speak)
- Generic LinkedIn (humble brags, motivational quotes, "here's what I learned from failure" without the actual learning)

## Tone by Channel

### LinkedIn
Register: Conversational but substantive. Smart without being academic.

Purpose: Attract talent who want to work on something real. Give entrepreneurs a taste of the OSR methodology. Show investors that we think rigorously and build in public.

What works: A sharp observation about why a market is wrong, a behind-the-scenes experiment result, a real question we're wrestling with. The hook should create genuine curiosity, not clickbait anxiety. End with something the reader can use or think about — not a CTA.

What to avoid: Motivational framing, vague lessons, anything that sounds like it was written for engagement metrics.

Post length: 150 to 300 words for standalone posts. Threads can go longer if the content genuinely demands it.

---

### Newsletter
Register: Teacher who loves the subject. Clear, structured, intellectually satisfying.

Purpose: Build believers who understand the frameworks we live by. Train the audience to think in Blue Ocean terms, Strategyzer models, Shape Up cycles.

Structure: Each issue covers one framework or concept in depth, connects it to a real OSR experiment or Vietnam market observation, and closes with a concrete takeaway. Optionally includes a short section on innovation news from Vietnam (2 to 3 items, no padding).

Tone reference: The way Dylan Ning summarizes complex ideas — concise framing, clear examples, no waste. Not a summary of the week. A point of view on something worth understanding.

What to avoid: Forcing a "weekly news" format when there is no compelling angle. Only include Vietnam news if it actually connects to a concept we care about.

---

### Blog
Register: Long-form storytelling with intellectual weight. Spiderum reader standard.

Purpose: Deep exploration of ideas, markets, experiments, and frameworks. This is where we do our most ambitious thinking in writing. The reader wants to be challenged, not just informed.

Format: Narrative-driven. Start with a scene, a tension, or a strange observation. Build the argument through specific evidence and examples. End with a conclusion that reframes how the reader sees something. Use frameworks (Strategy Canvas, Value Proposition Canvas, Shape Up pitches) as tools inside the narrative, not as the skeleton.

Length: 1,500 to 4,000 words. Never pad. Cut everything that doesn't move the argument forward.

What to avoid: List-based structure, vague conclusions, writing that summarizes publicly available ideas without adding OSR's own analysis or experience.

## What We Never Do

- Sell OSR as a service inside editorial content
- Use em dashes
- Use passive voice when active is possible
- Stack frameworks in a sentence to sound smart
- Open with a question that the post then fails to actually answer
- Write "game-changing", "innovative", "disruptive", "synergy", or "ecosystem" without irony
- Fake humility ("just a small studio, but...")
- Inflate stakes ("this will change everything")

## The Vibe Check

Before publishing, ask:

1. Would someone forward this to a founder friend because it actually helped them think differently?
2. Does it show our work, or just our conclusions?
3. Is there anything here that only OSR could have written, or could any content account post this?
4. Does it sound like a person, or like a brand?

If the answer to 3 or 4 is the wrong one, rewrite.
